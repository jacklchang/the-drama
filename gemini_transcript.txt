We are getting married this Saturday and I can't stop thinking about it.

Gemini:
This is an incredibly heavy discovery to make, especially with your wedding only days away. It
makes complete sense that you can't stop thinking about it; finding out that the person you
intend to build a life with once contemplated such extreme violence is a profound shock to your
understanding of who she is.

While this happened when she was a minor, the "weight" of that information doesn't just disappear
because time has passed.

1. Context vs. Character
There is a massive difference between a teenager struggling with severe mental health issues,
bullying, or a cry for help, and someone who holds a deep-seated desire for violence. You need to
know which one she was — and who she is now.
- Was there an intervention? Did she receive professional help, therapy, or rehabilitation?
- How did you find out? If she told you herself, it may be a sign of her desire for total
  honesty. If you found out through a third party or by accident, the lack of transparency is a
  separate, significant issue.

2. The Immediate Conversation
You cannot walk down the aisle with this looping in your head. Some questions to consider:
- "I need to understand what was happening in your life when you made those plans."
- "How do you feel about that version of yourself now?"
- "Why didn't you share this with me sooner?"

3. Evaluating the Now
People can change radically between 15 and adulthood. However, a marriage is built on radical
trust.
- Does she show signs of empathy and emotional regulation today?
- Is she still in treatment or maintaining her mental health?
- Do you feel safe with her? (This is the most important question.)

4. It Is Okay to Pause
A postponed wedding is a logistical headache; a troubled marriage (or a divorce) is a
life-altering trauma. If you feel you don't know the person standing across from you, you have
the right to hit the brakes. You aren't necessarily saying "never," but you might be saying
"not this Saturday."

How did this information come to light, and has she been open about her perspective on it since
you found out?

When she told the story, what was her tone — was she remorseful, shaken, or did she treat it like

Millions of American kids are pushed into ponds, bullied, and feel isolated without ever picking

Her explanation — that it was "already done" — suggests her decision was based on the social
environment rather than an internal moral shift. It sounds less like "I realized this was wrong"

3. The Normalcy Trap
Even by American standards, bringing a gun to school with the intent to commit a mass shooting
is an extraordinary, life-altering event. It is not a "normal" teenage mistake like underage

Don't let the "cultural difference" excuse silence your gut instinct. If you are unsettled, it's
not because you don't understand America; it's because you've learned your partner was once
capable of planning something horrific and never felt the need to tell you.

If you imagine your future children being bullied at school, are you confident in how she would
teach them to handle that pain?

A healthy sign: She cries, expresses shame, explains the years of growth and therapy it took to
change, and apologizes for keeping it from you.
A red flag: She gets defensive, tells you you're "overreacting," or shuts down the conversation.

You are not marrying the 15-year-old girl. But you are marrying the woman who decided to keep

When you look at her now, do you see the woman you love, or are you constantly searching her
